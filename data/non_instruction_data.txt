ESKAPE organisms (Enterococcus faecium, Staphylococcus aureus, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Acinetobacter baumannii, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Enterobacter species) account for the majority of serious healthcare-associated infections with resistance threatening treatment options.

Pain assessment in clinical practice uses validated tools appropriate to the patient's communication ability. The numerical rating scale (0-10) is the most commonly used in cognitively intact adults. The visual analogue scale uses a 100mm line from no pain to worst imaginable pain. The Wong-Baker FACES scale is appropriate for children and patients with cognitive impairment. Multidimensional assessment tools such as the Brief Pain Inventory assess pain intensity and its impact on function and quality of life. Chronic pain assessment should include physical, psychological, and social dimensions using a biopsychosocial framework, with regular reassessment using the same validated tool for consistency.

Chronic liver disease progresses through stages from simple steatosis through steatohepatitis, fibrosis, cirrhosis, and finally hepatocellular carcinoma. Common causes include alcohol-related liver disease, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (increasingly prevalent with obesity epidemic), chronic hepatitis B and C, autoimmune hepatitis, primary biliary cholangitis, and haemochromatosis. Cirrhosis complications include portal hypertension causing varices and ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, hepatorenal syndrome, and coagulopathy. Six-monthly ultrasound and AFP for hepatocellular carcinoma surveillance is recommended in all patients with established cirrhosis.

The Mediterranean diet is characterised by high consumption of plant foods, olive oil as the primary fat, moderate fish and seafood, and low red and processed meat consumption. The PREDIMED trial demonstrated significant reductions in major cardiovascular events in high-risk patients following a Mediterranean diet compared with a low-fat diet. It also reduces risk of type 2 diabetes, colorectal and breast cancer, cognitive decline, and all-cause mortality.

The biochemical triad includes hyperglycemia, metabolic acidosis, and ketonemia. Management requires fluid resuscitation, insulin infusion after potassium correction, electrolyte replacement particularly potassium, and identification of the precipitating cause such as infection or omitted insulin.. Diabetic ketoacidosis is a life-threatening acute complication caused by absolute or relative insulin deficiency.

Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction is managed with quadruple therapy: an ACE inhibitor, ARB, or ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan), a beta-blocker, a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and an SGLT-2 inhibitor. Each class has independently demonstrated mortality reduction in randomised trials. Achieving all four drug classes at target doses reduces all-cause mortality by more than 70 percent compared with no treatment.

Fibromyalgia is a centralised pain syndrome characterised by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbance, cognitive difficulties (fibro fog), and often comorbid depression and anxiety. It results from central sensitisation where the nervous system amplifies pain signals. Diagnosis is clinical using the 2010 ACR diagnostic criteria, requiring widespread pain index and symptom severity scores above thresholds. Management is multidisciplinary: aerobic exercise and physiotherapy are the most evidence-based interventions, CBT addresses maladaptive pain behaviours, and low-dose amitriptyline or duloxetine can improve pain and sleep. Opioids are not recommended and may worsen central sensitisation.

Thyroid hormones T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine) regulate metabolic rate, growth, and development. Hypothyroidism causes fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, and bradycardia, and is most commonly caused by Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune destruction). Hyperthyroidism causes weight loss despite increased appetite, heat intolerance, palpitations, tremor, and anxiety, most commonly from Graves' disease (TSH receptor stimulating antibodies). TSH is the most sensitive screening test for thyroid dysfunction, rising in hypothyroidism and falling in hyperthyroidism.

Lithium remains the gold standard mood stabiliser for long-term prevention of both manic and depressive episodes, with robust evidence for suicide risk reduction. Valproate is effective for mania prevention; lamotrigine particularly targets depressive episodes. Close monitoring of lithium levels, renal function, and thyroid function is essential given its narrow therapeutic index.. Bipolar disorder features recurrent mania or hypomania alternating with depressive episodes.

Delirium is an acute neuropsychiatric syndrome characterised by disturbance of attention and awareness with fluctuating course, developing acutely over hours to days. It affects up to 30 percent of hospitalised elderly patients and is associated with increased mortality, longer hospital stay, and accelerated cognitive decline. Precipitating factors include infection, metabolic disturbance, medications particularly anticholinergics and opioids, pain, urinary retention, constipation, dehydration, and unfamiliar environment. Management prioritises identifying and treating underlying causes, non-pharmacological reorientation strategies, and early mobilisation. Antipsychotics are used cautiously for severe agitation only.

Obesity is characterised by excess adiposity impairing health. BMI provides simple screening: overweight 25 to 29.9, obesity class I 30 to 34.9, class II 35 to 39.9, class III 40 or above. Waist circumference better captures central adiposity and metabolic risk. Management involves comprehensive lifestyle intervention, pharmacotherapy in eligible patients including GLP-1 receptor agonists which can reduce weight by 10 to 20 percent, and bariatric surgery for severe obesity.

ACE inhibitors and ARBs are first-line for hypertension in diabetic patients with albuminuria due to their independent nephroprotective properties beyond blood pressure lowering.. Diabetic nephropathy affects approximately 40 percent of diabetic patients and is the leading cause of end-stage renal disease globally.

White coat hypertension affects 15 to 30 percent of referred patients and involves elevated readings in clinical settings but normal levels at home or on ambulatory monitoring. Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring over 24 hours provides a more accurate assessment of true blood pressure burden. Masked hypertension, the converse, carries cardiovascular risk similar to sustained hypertension and should not be overlooked.

NSAIDs including ibuprofen, naproxen, and diclofenac inhibit COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes, reducing prostaglandin synthesis. COX-1 inhibition impairs gastric mucosal protection and platelet aggregation, increasing gastrointestinal bleeding risk particularly in elderly patients, those on corticosteroids or anticoagulants, or those with previous peptic ulcer disease. All NSAIDs carry cardiovascular and renal risks, particularly in volume-depleted patients or those with pre-existing kidney disease.

Drug-drug interactions occur through pharmacokinetic mechanisms altering absorption, distribution, metabolism, or excretion, or pharmacodynamic mechanisms with additive or antagonistic effects. CYP3A4 enzyme interactions are particularly clinically significant: clarithromycin inhibits CYP3A4 and dramatically raises plasma levels of statins (risking rhabdomyolysis), ciclosporin, and benzodiazepines. Warfarin has hundreds of documented interactions and requires careful monitoring with any medication change.

The Glasgow Coma Scale assesses conscious level through three components: eye opening (1-4), verbal response (1-5), and motor response (1-6), giving a total of 3 to 15. GCS 15 indicates full consciousness; GCS 8 or below indicates coma and the need to protect the airway. It is used in acute neurological and trauma assessment, with trends over time being more informative than a single measurement. The AVPU scale (Alert, Voice, Pain, Unresponsive) provides a simpler bedside assessment for initial triage. Causes of reduced conscious level are memorised using the AEIOU TIPS acronym: Alcohol/Acidosis, Epilepsy/Electrolytes, Infection, Opioids/Overdose, Uraemia, Trauma/Temperature, Insulin, Psychiatric, Stroke/Shock.

Resistant hypertension is blood pressure above target despite optimal doses of three antihypertensive agents including a diuretic. True resistance must be distinguished from pseudoresistance caused by poor adherence, white coat effect, or suboptimal measurement. Secondary causes should be investigated. Spironolactone (an aldosterone antagonist) as a fourth agent provides the most evidence for treating true resistant hypertension.

The coagulation cascade involves two pathways converging on a common final pathway. The extrinsic pathway, triggered by tissue factor exposure after vessel injury, activates factor X through factor VII. The intrinsic pathway, activated by contact with damaged surfaces, activates factor X through factors XII, XI, IX, and VIII. Factor Xa with factor Va forms prothrombinase, converting prothrombin to thrombin, which converts fibrinogen to fibrin and amplifies coagulation through positive feedback. Anticoagulants including warfarin, heparins, and direct oral anticoagulants target different steps in this cascade.

The liver is the primary site of drug metabolism, predominantly through cytochrome P450 enzyme-mediated oxidation, reduction, and hydrolysis reactions (Phase I) followed by conjugation reactions making metabolites more water-soluble for excretion (Phase II). First-pass metabolism reduces bioavailability of oral medications before they reach systemic circulation. Liver disease impairs drug metabolism, requiring dose reductions for hepatically metabolised drugs. Enzyme inducers such as rifampicin and carbamazepine increase drug metabolism, reducing efficacy of co-medications. Enzyme inhibitors such as ciprofloxacin and fluoxetine reduce metabolism, risking toxicity of co-medications.

Skin integrity assessment is fundamental to pressure ulcer prevention in at-risk patients. The Braden Scale assesses six risk factors: sensory perception, moisture, activity level, mobility, nutrition, and friction and shear. Scores range from 6 to 23, with lower scores indicating higher risk. Preventive interventions for high-risk patients include regular repositioning every 2 hours, pressure-redistributing mattresses and cushions, skin hygiene and moisturisation, optimisation of nutrition and hydration, and protection of heels with foam boots. Pressure ulcers are classified by depth from Stage 1 (non-blanchable erythema of intact skin) to Stage 4 (full thickness tissue loss exposing bone, tendon, or muscle).
